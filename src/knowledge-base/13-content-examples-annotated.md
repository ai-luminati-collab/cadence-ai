# 13 — Annotated Content Examples (Great vs. Bad)

> This file teaches the AI by SHOWING, not just telling.
> The AI learns significantly more from annotated examples than from abstract rules.

---

## INSTAGRAM REELS

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Hook**: "Nobody talks about the real reason your reels die in 3 seconds"
**Why it works**: Creates a curiosity gap (what IS the real reason?), implies insider knowledge, uses specific metric ("3 seconds") that the viewer recognizes from their own experience. Feels like a friend sharing a secret, not a brand lecturing.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Hook**: "5 Tips to Make Your Instagram Reels Go Viral in 2026!"
**Why it fails**: "Tips to go viral" is the most saturated hook format on Instagram. No curiosity gap — the viewer already knows what's coming (a listicle). The exclamation mark feels desperate. "Go viral" is an empty promise that users have been desensitized to.

## INSTAGRAM CAROUSELS

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Cover Slide**: "Stop using these 5 Instagram hashtags"
**Why it works**: Creates a curiosity gap (which 5?), implies the reader might be making a mistake (loss aversion), uses direct address ("you"), and the number 5 promises a scannable list. The viewer MUST swipe to discover which hashtags.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Cover Slide**: "Top 5 Instagram Hashtag Tips for 2026"
**Why it fails**: No curiosity gap — the reader already knows the topic. No emotional trigger. Generic phrasing that could be on any marketing blog. "Tips for 2026" is a format the audience has seen thousands of times.

### GREAT EXAMPLE (D2C Product)
**Cover Slide**: "The ₹12 spice that Mumbai's top chefs refuse to name"
**Why it works**: Price specificity creates intrigue ("only ₹12?"), authority borrowed from "top chefs," exclusivity from "refuse to name." The viewer swipes to discover WHICH spice, and feels like they're getting insider access.

### BAD EXAMPLE (D2C Product)
**Cover Slide**: "Authentic Indian Spices — Why Quality Matters"
**Why it fails**: "Authentic" is a banned AI smog word. "Why Quality Matters" is the most generic value proposition possible. No specific claim, no curiosity gap, no reason to swipe.

## LINKEDIN TEXT POSTS

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Opening**: "I lost ₹40 lakhs on my first marketing campaign. Here's what nobody warned me about"
**Why it works**: Specific financial loss creates immediate empathy and credibility. "Nobody warned me about" creates a curiosity gap. Opens with vulnerability, not authority. The reader thinks "I don't want to make the same mistake" — that's loss aversion at work.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Opening**: "I'm excited to announce that I've been promoted to Head of Marketing at XYZ Corp!"
**Why it fails**: This opening signals corporate communication, not personal insight. LinkedIn's algorithm deprioritizes content that reads like a press release. Users have been trained to scroll past "excited to announce" openings.

### GREAT EXAMPLE (B2B)
**Opening**: "Our biggest client just fired us. Best thing that ever happened."
**Why it works**: Contradicts expectations (being fired = good?). The reader MUST continue to understand the paradox. Feels honest and anti-corporate. Sets up a story arc.

### BAD EXAMPLE (B2B)
**Opening**: "At our company, we believe in delivering world-class solutions that transform businesses."
**Why it fails**: Pure corporate jargon. "World-class," "solutions," and "transform" are all AI smog words. Says nothing specific or memorable. Could be any company in any industry.

## X (TWITTER) POSTS

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Tweet**: "hot take: the brands that post 3x a day on X are just doing group therapy in public"
**Why it works**: Contrarian ("hot take"), uses humor to land a real insight (over-posting = insecurity), relatable observation that the audience can share. Short, punchy, quotable.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Tweet**: "Consistency is key! 🔑 Make sure you're posting regularly to build your brand. #MarketingTips #SocialMedia"
**Why it fails**: Cliché opening ("Consistency is key"), hashtag spam, generic advice that adds zero value. The emoji usage feels performative. Nobody quote-tweets this.

### GREAT EXAMPLE (D2C Spice Brand)
**Tweet**: "your masala dabba has more range than most content calendars"
**Why it works**: Bridges the audience's world (masala dabba = every Indian kitchen) with the brand's world (content). Witty comparison that makes people smile and tag friends. Cultural specificity = shareability.

### BAD EXAMPLE (D2C Spice Brand)
**Tweet**: "Experience the authentic taste of India with our premium spice range! 🌶️ Shop now: [link] #IndianSpices #PremiumFood"
**Why it fails**: "Experience," "authentic," "premium" are all smog words. Hard-sell CTA with no value. Hashtags are generic. Reads like a Google Ad, not a tweet.

## INSTAGRAM STORIES

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Poll Story**: "Be honest: do you actually measure your masala or does your heart just KNOW? 🤌" [Poll: "I measure precisely" / "Andaaza gang 💪"]
**Why it works**: Relatable truth about Indian cooking, inclusive humor, the poll options are funny and shareable. "Andaaza gang" creates in-group identity. Low effort to engage.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Story**: "Check out our latest product range! Swipe up to shop now! 🛒"
**Why it fails**: Zero engagement mechanism. No reason for the viewer to respond. Pure advertisement with no entertainment or value exchange.

---

## YOUTUBE SHORTS

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Hook**: [Visual: Close-up of oil crackling in a kadhai] "The sound that tells you your tadka is LYING to you"
**Why it works**: Opens with a sensory trigger (sound), creates immediate tension ("lying to you?"), addresses a specific cooking anxiety. The viewer watches to learn what the "lie" is.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Hook**: "Here are 3 tips for making the perfect tadka"
**Why it fails**: "Here are 3 tips" is the laziest hook format. No curiosity gap, no tension, no reason to watch. The viewer can guess the tips without watching.

## PINTEREST

### GREAT EXAMPLE
**Pin Title**: "The Mumbai street food spice ratio that nobody will teach you"
**Why it works**: Specificity (Mumbai street food), exclusivity (nobody will teach you), implies a secret worth saving. Pinterest users save actionable, specific content.

### BAD EXAMPLE
**Pin Title**: "Indian Cooking Tips and Tricks"
**Why it fails**: Too broad, no specificity, no reason to save. Pinterest's algorithm rewards specific, actionable titles. This is a category label, not a content piece.
