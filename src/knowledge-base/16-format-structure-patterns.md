# Format Structure Patterns

*Structural intelligence for the Generation Engine. Each entry decodes how a specific content format should be built — the anatomy, pacing, hook mechanics, and closing logic that make the format work. These are architectural blueprints, not templates.*

**How to use this library:** When the Generation Engine receives a format assignment (carousel, reel, static, etc.), it loads the corresponding structure pattern. The pattern tells the Worker how to architect the piece — what each section needs to accomplish, how information should flow, and where the structural pitfalls are. The Boss uses the same patterns to audit structural integrity: is the carousel actually built like a good carousel, or is it a list of slides?

**Critical principle:** These are structural patterns, not scripts. "Open with a contradictory claim" is a structure. "Did you know that [product] can [benefit]?" is a script. The former transfers across brands and topics. The latter produces sameness. The Generation Engine should use these patterns to build structure, then fill the structure with brand-specific content, tone, and strategic intent from the other libraries.

**Structure:** 10 format patterns covering the primary content formats for social-first brands.

## Format 01: Instagram Carousel

### What it is
A swipeable sequence of 2-20 slides (images or graphics) with an optional caption. The format's core mechanic is sequential revelation — each slide earns the next swipe. The carousel is Instagram's highest-save and highest-share format because it delivers dense value in a compact, browseable structure.

### Structural anatomy

**Slide 1 — The Hook Slide.** This is the only slide the audience sees in the feed. Its entire job is to earn the first swipe. It must create a gap — curiosity, tension, surprise, or promise — that the remaining slides close. It should NOT be a title card ("5 Tips for Better Marketing"). It should be a hook that works visually and textually in under 1 second.

Strong Slide 1 patterns:
- Contradictory claim: "Everything you know about [topic] is wrong"
- Specific provocation: "We lost $40K last quarter because of this one mistake"
- Outcome-first: "This framework took us from 200 to 10,000 followers in 60 days"
- Anti-expectation: "Stop posting reels" (from a social media brand)

**Slides 2-8 — The Body.** Each slide should deliver one idea. Not a sentence fragment, not a paragraph — one complete idea that the reader absorbs in 2-3 seconds. Body slides build on each other sequentially. Slide 3 should assume the reader has seen slides 1-2. This creates a cumulative effect that isolated slides can't.

Body slide principles:
- One idea per slide, developed enough to be satisfying but brief enough to earn the swipe
- Each slide should create a micro-reason to see the next (a setup, an open question, an "and..." energy)
- Visual hierarchy matters: the most important text should be largest, supporting text smaller
- Consistency of format — if slide 2 is a bold claim + explanation, slides 3-7 should follow the same visual grammar

**Slide 9-10 — The Payoff + Close.** The second-to-last slide delivers the final insight, synthesis, or emotional payoff. The last slide closes with a CTA that matches the content's energy (not a generic "Follow for more"). The payoff slide is the most important slide after slide 1 — it's what makes the audience feel the carousel was worth their time.

### Pacing logic
A strong carousel follows an energy curve: high opening (hook) → building middle (each slide adds value) → peak (the strongest insight, usually at slide 7-8) → resolution (synthesis + CTA). The worst carousels are flat — every slide at the same energy level, like a list. The best carousels feel like a story with a beginning, escalation, and payoff.

### Closing mechanics
The final slide should do ONE of:
- **Synthesize:** Pull the carousel's thread together into a single takeaway
- **Provoke:** Leave the audience with a question or tension that extends beyond the carousel
- **Redirect:** Point the audience toward a specific next action that's earned by the content

Never: logo card with "Follow for more." Never: the same visual format as body slides with nothing new to say. The close should feel like a deliberate ending, not the place where slides ran out.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Everything Carousel (Anti-Pattern #08): Too many topics, too little depth
- The Orphan Insight (Anti-Pattern #14): Best slide buried in the middle
- The Throat-Clear (Anti-Pattern #02): Slide 1 is a topic label, not a hook
- The Infinite Scroll (Anti-Pattern #10): No payoff, just a list that stops

### Benchmarks
- Sweet spot: 7-10 slides. Under 5 feels thin. Over 10 loses people unless every slide is exceptional.
- Text per slide: 15-40 words for hook/insight slides. Up to 60 for explanation slides. Over 60 = Wall of Value.
- Slide 1 text: under 15 words. This is a hook, not an abstract.
- Caption: 50-150 words. The carousel carries the value; the caption adds context, personality, or CTA. Don't repeat the carousel content in the caption.

## Format 02: Single Static + Caption

### What it is
A single image (photograph, graphic, or text-on-image) paired with a caption. The simplest Instagram format and the hardest to do well because there's no sequence to build through — a single frame must carry the entire message. The format rewards density of thought and visual impact.

### Structural anatomy

**The Image.** Either carries the message itself (text-on-image, quote graphic, data visualization) or sets a visual context that the caption completes (product shot, scene, photograph). The image must stop the scroll on its own — it gets 0.3-0.5 seconds of attention in the feed. If it's a text-on-image, the text must be readable at phone scale and say something worth reading in under 2 seconds.

Strong image patterns:
- Text-on-image with a single sharp sentence (works like a print ad headline)
- Product in an unexpected context (creates curiosity: what's happening here?)
- Data point or statistic rendered visually (graph, chart, or bold number)
- Screenshot or raw texture (anti-polish as a scroll-stopper)

**The Caption.** The caption's role depends on the image. If the image carries the message, the caption adds depth, context, or personality. If the image sets a mood or context, the caption delivers the substance. The caption should not repeat what the image already says — the two should work as complements, not echoes.

Caption structure for substance posts:
- Opening line: the hook. Visible in the feed preview (first ~125 characters). Must earn the "...more" tap.
- Body: 2-4 short paragraphs developing the idea. Each paragraph should stand alone as a readable unit.
- Close: single-line CTA or closing thought. Earned by the body, not bolted on.

Caption structure for mood/lifestyle posts:
- Short, specific, voice-forward. 1-3 sentences that add personality without over-explaining the image.
- The caption makes the audience hear the brand, not just see it.

### Pacing logic
No sequence to pace, but the image-caption relationship has its own rhythm. The image should do 50-70% of the attention-grabbing work. The caption should do 50-70% of the meaning-making work. Neither should carry both loads alone.

### Closing mechanics
For caption-heavy posts: end on a question that invites genuine response, a provocation that leaves residue, or a sharp callback to the opening. For short-caption posts: end when you're done. One clean sentence. Resist the urge to add a CTA to every static.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Pretty Nothing (image is beautiful, caption is "✨"): all visual, no substance
- The Caption Essay (caption is 500 words, image is an afterthought): wrong format, should be a carousel
- The Image-Caption Echo (both say the same thing): wasted surface area
- The Hashtag Grave (Anti-Pattern #29): 30 hashtags burying a 2-line caption

### Benchmarks
- Caption sweet spot for substance: 80-200 words. Over 200, consider carousel instead.
- Caption for mood/lifestyle: 10-40 words.
- Text-on-image: under 15 words. If it needs more, it's a carousel slide, not a static.
- First line of caption: under 125 characters (the feed preview cutoff).

## Format 03: Reel / Short-Form Video

### What it is
Vertical video, 15-90 seconds (sweet spot: 30-60 seconds). The format's core mechanic is retention — the algorithm measures how much of the video the viewer watches. Every second must earn the next second. The format rewards energy, pacing, and pattern-interrupts. It punishes slow starts, predictable structure, and low visual variety.

### Structural anatomy

**Beat 1 — The Hook (0-1.5 seconds).** The most important 1.5 seconds in all of social content. The hook must accomplish one thing: stop the scroll. This means visual motion, text overlay, or audio that's immediately arresting. Not a logo. Not "Hey guys." Not a slow zoom-in. A disruption.

Strong hook patterns:
- Cold-open into the middle of an action or statement (no setup, no intro)
- Text overlay with a contradictory or surprising claim (visible before audio registers)
- Pattern-interrupt visual: unexpected close-up, sudden movement, jarring color
- Voice-first hook: the first spoken word is the sharpest word in the script

**Beat 2 — The Setup (1.5-10 seconds).** Establish what this reel is about. If the hook was a provocative claim, the setup is: "Here's what I mean." If the hook was a question, the setup is the context. The setup must maintain the momentum of the hook while adding enough context for the payoff to land.

**Beat 3 — The Body (10-40 seconds).** The substance. In a tutorial, this is the demonstration. In a story, this is the narrative. In an opinion piece, this is the argument. Key pacing principle: vary the shot type, angle, or visual every 3-5 seconds. Static shots beyond 5 seconds lose viewers. Cut, zoom, pan, or add a text overlay to maintain visual interest.

**Beat 4 — The Payoff (last 5-10 seconds).** The moment the reel delivers on the hook's promise. The insight, the punchline, the reveal, the result. This should be the most memorable moment. If it's not, restructure so it is. The payoff determines whether the viewer replays, shares, or saves.

**Beat 5 (optional) — The CTA (final 2-3 seconds).** If the reel needs a CTA, it comes after the payoff, not instead of it. A single, clear CTA. Not three. The CTA should feel like a natural extension of the payoff.

### Pacing logic
Reels are the format most sensitive to pacing. The energy curve should be: high (hook) → slightly lower (setup) → building (body with visual variety) → peak (payoff) → resolution (CTA or ending). The most common pacing failure is a flat middle — 20 seconds of talking head at the same distance, same angle, same energy. The fix is visual variety: cut to B-roll, add text overlay, change camera angle, or shift the speaker's position.

### Closing mechanics
Strong endings:
- Loop-worthy: the last frame connects to the first, creating a seamless loop (boosts algorithmic performance because replays count as retention)
- Hard cut: end on the strongest beat with no trailing. Cut to black or next scene immediately after the payoff.
- Callback: the last line references the hook, creating closure.

Weak endings:
- Trailing off: the speaker says the payoff and then keeps talking. Cut at the peak.
- The triple CTA: "Follow, like, share, and comment what you think!"
- The logo slate: a branded end card that nobody watches.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Throat-Clear hook: first 3 seconds are "Hey everyone, today I want to talk about..."
- The Flat Middle: 30 seconds of talking head, same angle, same energy
- The Premature Pivot (Anti-Pattern #09): relatable content → sudden product pitch
- No payoff: the reel delivers value but has no peak moment

### Benchmarks
- Optimal length: 30-60 seconds for most content. Under 15 for quick-hit humor or tips. Over 60 only when the content genuinely sustains attention.
- Hook window: 1.5 seconds maximum before the scroll-stopper hits.
- Visual variety: a new shot, angle, or text overlay every 3-5 seconds minimum.
- Text overlays: 3-8 words per overlay. Visible for 2-3 seconds minimum.
- Watch-through target: 60%+ average watch time for strong performance.

## Format 04: Instagram Story Sequence

### What it is
A sequence of full-screen vertical frames (image or video, 5-15 seconds each) that disappear after 24 hours. The format's core mechanic is intimacy and ephemerality — stories feel personal, immediate, and low-stakes. The swipe-through mechanic means each frame must earn the next tap. Stories reward personality, behind-the-scenes access, and interactive elements (polls, questions, sliders).

### Structural anatomy

**Frame 1 — The Attention Frame.** The first story frame must stop the viewer from tapping to the next account's stories. It has about 1 second. Strong openers: a text-over-photo with a provocative or curious statement, a quick video clip of something in progress, or a poll/question that immediately engages. Never: a brand logo, a "good morning" text, or a plain product photo with no context.

**Frames 2-4 — The Build.** Each frame adds one layer to the story being told. If frame 1 is "We almost cancelled our product launch this morning," frame 2 is "Here's what happened," frame 3 is the detail, frame 4 is the resolution. Each frame should feel like a natural continuation — the viewer should feel pulled forward, not forced.

**Frame 5-7 — The Payoff + Interactive.** The payoff (what the story was building toward) followed by an interactive element that turns passive viewing into active engagement. Polls, question stickers, sliders, and quizzes serve two purposes: they boost algorithmic ranking (the algorithm counts interactions) and they create a two-way relationship moment.

### Pacing logic
Stories are the fastest-paced format. Each frame gets 3-5 seconds of active attention. The sequence should feel like a conversation, not a presentation — casual, direct, slightly unpolished. The pacing should be: hook → context → development → payoff → interaction. Keep text short (under 20 words per frame for text overlays), use video where possible, and don't over-design — stories that look too produced feel wrong for the format.

### Closing mechanics
Stories don't need grand endings. The final frame should either:
- End on the interactive element (poll, question) — let the interaction be the close
- Direct to a specific action ("Swipe up" / "Link" for qualified audiences, or "Reply to this story" for community-building)
- Simply stop when the story is told. No summary frame, no logo. Ephemerality is the format's strength.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Broadcast Story: treating stories like a feed post (polished graphic, no personality, no interaction)
- The Story Dump: posting 15+ frames in one sequence, exhausting the viewer
- The Poll-for-Nothing: using interactive elements without a genuine reason (a poll that doesn't connect to any content)
- The Link-on-Everything: treating every story as a traffic-driving moment instead of a relationship-building moment

### Benchmarks
- Sweet spot: 3-7 frames per sequence. Over 10 and completion rate drops significantly.
- Text per frame: under 20 words for overlays. Under 30 for text-only frames.
- Interactive elements: at least 1 per sequence, positioned in the second half (after context is established).
- Video vs. image: video frames retain attention longer. Use at least 1-2 video frames per sequence.
- Posting frequency: 1-3 story sequences per day. More than that and each sequence competes with itself.

## Format 05: LinkedIn Text Post

### What it is
A text-first format (up to 3,000 characters) in a feed dominated by professionals in learning/networking mode. The format's core mechanic is the "...see more" fold — only the first 2-3 lines are visible in the feed. Everything after the fold is earned. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards dwell time (how long someone spends on the post), comments, and shares. The format privileges clear thinking over visual polish.

### Structural anatomy

**Line 1 — The Hook.** The single most important line. It appears in the feed above the fold. It must create a reason to tap "see more." Strong LinkedIn hooks are specific, surprising, or slightly uncomfortable. They work because LinkedIn's default content is vague and comfortable — any post that starts with a real claim or a specific number stands out.

Strong line 1 patterns:
- Specific number + unexpected context: "I fired our best-performing salesperson last month."
- Counterintuitive claim: "The worst career advice I ever took: follow your passion."
- Cold-open narrative: "3am. My phone buzzes. The Slack notification that changed everything."
- Direct challenge: "Most marketing teams measure the wrong things. Here's the one metric that matters."

**Lines 2-3 — The Bridge.** Visible right below the hook, before the fold. These lines must maintain the hook's momentum. They should add context, not backpedal. If line 1 is provocative, lines 2-3 should double down, not soften.

**The Body (below the fold).** The substance. LinkedIn body copy should be structured for scan-readability: short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each), line breaks between paragraphs, and occasional one-line paragraphs for emphasis. The body should develop the hook's premise with specificity — examples, data, narrative, or argument. Not platitudes.

Key body principles:
- Short paragraphs. 1-3 sentences maximum. Whitespace is your friend.
- Vary paragraph length. A one-sentence paragraph after a three-sentence paragraph creates rhythm.
- Use specific details. Names, numbers, dates, situations. Specificity is LinkedIn's rarest commodity.
- Avoid hedge language. "I think," "It's worth noting," "In my experience" — these weaken claims. State the claim. The audience can disagree.

**The Close.** The last 2-3 lines. Should feel intentional — a conclusion, not a trail-off. Strong closes either synthesize the post's argument into a single memorable line, or open a genuine question for discussion.

### Pacing logic
LinkedIn posts follow an hourglass: wide opening (provocative claim) → narrow middle (specific details, story, evidence) → wide closing (broader implication or question). The middle is where most posts fail — they make a strong claim in line 1 and then pad with generalities. The middle needs the same specificity as the hook.

### Closing mechanics
Strong closes:
- The reframe: "So the question isn't whether to hire for culture fit. It's whether your culture is worth fitting into."
- The honest question: "I'm not sure I got this right. What would you have done?" (genuine, not engagement-bait)
- The sharp callback: returning to line 1 with new meaning added by the body

Weak closes:
- "Agree? Disagree? Let me know in the comments!" (engagement bait)
- A generic takeaway that could end any post ("The lesson? Be authentic.")
- A self-promotional CTA disconnected from the content

### Common structural mistakes
- The Throat-Clear (Anti-Pattern #02): "In today's competitive landscape..." — the LinkedIn default opening
- The LinkedIn Humble-Brag: "I'm so humbled to announce..." (the audience sees through this instantly)
- The Framework Force (Anti-Pattern #04): Every insight packaged as "My 3-Step Framework for X"
- The Wall of Value (Anti-Pattern #11): One continuous paragraph of 500 words with no line breaks

### Benchmarks
- Sweet spot: 800-1,500 characters. Under 500 feels thin for LinkedIn's format. Over 2,000 risks losing attention.
- Line 1: under 80 characters. Sharp, specific, complete.
- Paragraphs: 1-3 sentences. Never more than 4 sentences without a line break.
- Questions in the post: 0-1 genuine questions. More than 1 feels like an interrogation.
- Emoji usage: 0-1 per post on LinkedIn. More signals low-quality content (Anti-Pattern #37).

## Format 06: LinkedIn Carousel / Document Post

### What it is
A PDF or multi-page document uploaded to LinkedIn that displays as a swipeable slide deck in the feed. Functionally similar to an Instagram carousel but in a professional context with a different audience mindset. LinkedIn carousels consistently outperform text posts for saves and shares because they deliver structured, referenceable value. The format rewards clear frameworks, visual organization, and actionable depth.

### Structural anatomy

**Slide 1 — The Title/Hook Slide.** Same principle as Instagram carousel slide 1, but the audience is in a professional learning mindset. The hook should signal "this will make you better at your job" or "this challenges something you assume." LinkedIn hooks skew more toward professional provocation and data than Instagram hooks.

Strong slide 1 patterns:
- Outcome-driven: "The hiring process that reduced our attrition by 40%"
- Myth-busting: "3 'best practices' in marketing that are actually wrong"
- Framework reveal: "The one-page strategy doc we use for every product launch" (with the actual doc visible, not a teaser)
- Data-driven: "We analyzed 500 LinkedIn posts. Here's what actually gets engagement."

**Slides 2-8 — The Content Slides.** LinkedIn carousels work best when they deliver a single framework, process, or argument across the sequence. Each slide is one step, one data point, or one argument in the sequence. Higher text tolerance than Instagram (LinkedIn audience will read 40-60 words per slide) but visual hierarchy still matters.

**Slide 9-10 — Summary + CTA.** LinkedIn carousels benefit from a summary slide more than Instagram carousels do, because the audience treats them as reference material. A clean summary of the key points gives the audience a reason to save. The CTA should connect to professional context: "Follow for more frameworks like this" or "Repost if your team should see this."

### Pacing logic
LinkedIn carousels should feel like a well-structured presentation, not a creative piece. Logical flow matters more than emotional arc. The sequence should follow: premise (slide 1) → context/why this matters (slide 2) → the framework/process/argument (slides 3-8) → summary/synthesis (slide 9) → CTA (slide 10).

### Closing mechanics
LinkedIn audiences save carousels as reference material. The close should make saving worthwhile: a summary slide, a checklist, or a "Do this next Monday" action item. Professional context makes action-oriented closes more effective than reflective ones.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Slide Deck Port: a presentation deck uploaded as-is, with no adaptation for the feed context (too many words, no hook, assumes a meeting room)
- The Everything Carousel (Anti-Pattern #08): trying to cover an entire topic instead of one specific angle
- The Listicle Carousel: every slide is a tip, no structure building between slides, no cumulative insight

### Benchmarks
- Sweet spot: 8-12 slides. LinkedIn audience has slightly higher patience than Instagram.
- Text per slide: 30-60 words. LinkedIn audiences will read more, but visual hierarchy must prioritize key phrases.
- Design: clean, professional, consistent. LinkedIn carousels should look like they came from someone who thinks clearly, not from a designer showing off.
- Caption: 100-300 words. LinkedIn captions complement the carousel — add the "why" and the personal angle.

## Format 07: X / Twitter Thread

### What it is
A sequence of connected tweets (now posts on X), each up to 280 characters. The format's core mechanic is progressive disclosure — each tweet reveals one layer, creating a pull-through effect. Threads reward sharp thinking expressed concisely. The format is one of the few places where text alone, without any visual support, can go viral on the strength of ideas.

### Structural anatomy

**Tweet 1 — The Hook.** The only tweet the audience sees in their feed. Its job is to earn the "Show this thread" click. The hook must work as a standalone statement AND as an invitation to read more. It must create an open loop.

Strong tweet 1 patterns:
- Bold claim + implicit promise: "The best brands in the world do something that most marketers would never try. Thread."
- Specific number: "I spent $100K on Facebook ads last year. Here's exactly where every dollar went."
- Counterintuitive: "You shouldn't post every day on Instagram. Here's why."
- Story opening: "Last year I almost shut down my company. What saved us was the stupidest idea we ever had."

**Tweets 2-8 — The Body.** Each tweet should deliver one complete thought. Not a sentence fragment, not half an idea continued in the next tweet. Each tweet should be readable in isolation while also building on the previous one. This dual requirement — standalone value + sequential building — is what makes threads hard and what separates good threads from mediocre ones.

Body tweet principles:
- One idea per tweet. If you need two tweets for one idea, the idea is too complex for this format or needs editing.
- Vary the structure. Short declarative tweet → longer explanatory tweet → example tweet → one-line observation. Rhythm variation keeps the reader engaged.
- Use line breaks within tweets for readability. A single 280-character block is harder to scan than two short lines.

**Tweet 9-10 — The Close.** Threads need endings. The final tweet should synthesize, provoke, or redirect. It should feel like the thread arrived somewhere — not just ran out of tweets.

### Pacing logic
Threads should escalate. Each tweet should be at least as interesting as the previous one. If the thread peaks at tweet 3 and coasts through tweets 4-8, it needs restructuring. The best threads build tension through the middle and deliver their strongest insight in the final third.

### Closing mechanics
Strong thread endings:
- The synthesis tweet: pulls the thread together into one sharp conclusion
- The reframe: the last tweet recasts everything above in a new light
- The call-to-contribute: "What would you add?" (genuine, not engagement-bait, after a thread that's earned the question)

Weak thread endings:
- "If you enjoyed this, follow me for more threads like this 🧵" (the thread equivalent of begging)
- A self-promotional plug disconnected from the thread content
- The thread just stops because you ran out of points

### Common structural mistakes
- The Sentence Fragments: breaking one thought across 3 tweets for artificial length. Each tweet must standalone.
- The List Thread: tweet 1 hook, then tweets 2-10 are a list with no narrative or building logic
- Thread bloat: 20+ tweets when 8 would carry the same insight. Padding is more visible in threads than any other format.
- No payoff: the hook promises a revelation, the thread delivers common knowledge

### Benchmarks
- Sweet spot: 5-12 tweets. Under 5 often doesn't justify the thread format. Over 15 risks losing the audience.
- Characters per tweet: use the full 280 when needed, but don't pad to fill. Some of the strongest tweets in a thread are under 100 characters.
- Tweet 1: should work as a standalone tweet. Test: would it earn engagement without the thread?
- Thread frequency: 1-2 per week maximum. Threads that appear daily lose their "this is worth reading" signal.

## Format 08: YouTube Short

### What it is
Vertical short-form video, up to 60 seconds. Mechanically similar to Reels and TikTok but with a distinct audience context: YouTube's audience skews more toward information-seeking and entertainment than Instagram's aesthetic-social context. YouTube Shorts also benefit from YouTube's search infrastructure — Shorts can be discovered through search, not just feed.

### Structural anatomy

**Second 0-1 — The Visual Hook.** Identical priority to Reels: stop the scroll in under 1 second. YouTube Shorts compete with a feed full of thumbnails and fast cuts. The hook must be visual and immediate.

**Seconds 1-5 — The Promise.** What is this Short about and why should the viewer stay? This is slightly more explicit than Reels because YouTube's audience is more intentional — they want to know what they're getting. A quick text overlay or verbal statement: "Here's how to [specific outcome]" or "This is the [specific thing] nobody talks about."

**Seconds 5-45 — The Delivery.** Denser and more information-forward than Reels. YouTube's audience tolerates more teaching and less entertainment per second than Instagram's. But visual variety still matters: cut every 3-5 seconds, use text overlays for key points, and show rather than tell where possible.

**Seconds 45-60 — The Close.** YouTube Shorts that end with a reason to watch again or subscribe perform best. The close should deliver the payoff and then end cleanly. YouTube's algorithm rewards replays, so loop-worthy endings have structural value.

### Pacing logic
YouTube Shorts can be slightly more deliberate than Reels because the audience is in a different mode (learning/entertainment vs. browsing/social). But only slightly. The same principle applies: every second earns the next. Vary visual input every 3-5 seconds. Use text overlays to reinforce key spoken points.

### Closing mechanics
YouTube-specific advantage: Shorts can drive to long-form content. "Full breakdown in the video linked above" (YouTube sometimes links Shorts to related long-form content). This makes Shorts a legitimate top-of-funnel for a YouTube channel in a way that Reels can't be for an Instagram profile.

### Common structural mistakes
- Treating Shorts as "clipped long-form": cutting a section of a long video and posting it as a Short without restructuring for the format
- Missing the hook window: YouTube Shorts are consumed in a fast-scroll feed, same as Reels. The first second matters as much.
- Over-teaching: YouTube audience will learn from Shorts, but not from lectures. Dense information needs visual support.

### Benchmarks
- Optimal length: 30-50 seconds for informational Shorts. 15-30 for entertainment/humor.
- Hook window: 1 second maximum.
- Visual variety: new shot or overlay every 3-5 seconds.
- Text overlays: use for key terms, numbers, and takeaways. 3-6 words per overlay.

## Format 09: Long-Form Video Script (YouTube / Podcast Video)

### What it is
Horizontal video, typically 8-25 minutes for YouTube. The format's core mechanic is sustained attention — the video must earn the viewer's time across a long duration. YouTube's algorithm heavily weights watch time (total minutes watched) and audience retention (percentage of video watched). Long-form rewards depth, narrative structure, and genuine expertise.

### Structural anatomy

**The First 30 Seconds — The Hook + Preview.** YouTube gives you 30 seconds before the audience decides to stay or bounce. This window must accomplish: (1) hook with a compelling claim, question, or scene, (2) preview what the video will deliver, (3) establish why THIS video is worth the viewer's time on this topic. No channel intros, no "hey guys welcome back to my channel." Cold-open into the content.

**Minutes 1-3 — The Setup.** Context and stakes. Why does this topic matter? What's at stake? What will the viewer know by the end that they don't know now? The setup should create a knowledge gap or a tension that the rest of the video closes. This is also where the viewer commits — if the setup is compelling, they'll stay for the body.

**Minutes 3-15 — The Body.** The substance, organized into 3-5 clear sections. Each section should have its own mini-arc: setup → evidence/explanation → insight. Transitions between sections should be signposted (viewers need to know where they are in the video's structure). Use visual variety, B-roll, graphics, and on-screen text to support the spoken content.

Body pacing principles:
- Change the visual every 10-15 seconds (B-roll, graphic, screen recording, different camera angle)
- Signpost sections: "The first thing to understand is..." / "But here's where it gets interesting..."
- Build toward the strongest insight. If the best point is in section 2 of 5, restructure.
- Include "retention moments" — pattern-interrupts that re-engage viewers who are drifting (a surprising fact, a change in energy, a direct question to the viewer)

**Minutes 15-20 — The Payoff.** The video's main insight, delivered with the weight it deserves. This should feel like the moment the video was building toward. It should recontextualize what came before — the viewer should now see the earlier sections differently.

**Final 1-2 Minutes — The Close + CTA.** Synthesis, final thought, and a CTA that's earned. YouTube-specific: asking for subscribes and likes works here more than on other platforms because YouTube's audience has been trained to expect it. But the CTA should still feel earned and natural, not desperate.

### Pacing logic
Long-form video follows a macro energy curve across the full duration: hook (high) → setup (builds intrigue) → body sections (oscillating energy — each section has its own mini-arc) → payoff (peak) → close (resolution). Within the body, each section should have its own hook, build, and payoff. Think of it as a series of mini-videos connected by a single through-line.

### Closing mechanics
Long-form closes should feel conclusive. The viewer invested 15+ minutes. They deserve a sense of completion. The close should either synthesize (pull all sections together), reframe (cast the entire topic in a new light), or provoke (leave the viewer with a question that extends beyond the video). Then CTA. Then end. Don't trail off.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Slow Open: spending the first 2 minutes on introductions, channel updates, and sponsor reads before the content starts
- The Section Slog: body sections that are all the same length, same energy, same structure — no variation
- No signposting: the viewer doesn't know where they are in the video's structure
- All teaching, no narrative: information delivered as lecture rather than story. Even educational content benefits from narrative structure.
- No payoff: the video covers a topic thoroughly but never arrives at an insight that was worth the time investment

### Benchmarks
- Optimal length: 10-20 minutes for most educational/analysis content. 5-10 for quick explorations. 20-30 for deep dives.
- Hook window: 30 seconds maximum before the viewer must understand what the video delivers and why they should stay.
- Visual variety: a new visual element (cut, graphic, B-roll) every 10-15 seconds in the body.
- Audience retention target: 50%+ average at the midpoint. If retention drops below 40% at the midpoint, the structure needs work.
- Sections: 3-5 clearly defined sections in the body. Each with its own mini-arc.

## Format 10: Email / Newsletter

### What it is
Direct-to-inbox content. The format's core mechanic is consent — the reader chose to receive this, which changes the attention contract. Email readers are more patient than social audiences, more willing to read at length, and more receptive to depth. But they also unsubscribe if the content doesn't consistently deliver. The format rewards consistency, personality, and genuine value that can't be found on the brand's social feeds.

### Structural anatomy

**The Subject Line.** The email's hook. Opens are determined by subject line + sender name. Subject lines should be specific and curiosity-inducing, not salesy or clickbaity. The subject line's job is to earn the open, not describe the contents. "We got this wrong" outperforms "Our March Newsletter Update."

Strong subject line patterns:
- Specific and curious: "The $12,000 mistake in our pricing page"
- Direct and personal: "A question I keep getting asked"
- Unexpected: "We're raising prices. Here's why that's good for you."
- Simple and human: "Quick thing" / "Noticed something weird" / "Been thinking about this"

**The First Line.** Visible in email previews alongside the subject line. Must continue the subject line's energy, not repeat it. If the subject is "We got this wrong," the first line should immediately get into what was wrong — not "Hi [Name], welcome to our March newsletter."

**The Body.** Email bodies can be longer than any social format. 500-1,500 words is a comfortable range for newsletter content. But length must be earned through substance, not padding. Structure the body with clear section breaks, short paragraphs, and one main thread that the reader can follow.

Body structure patterns:
- **The Single Story:** One narrative, told with detail and reflection. Best for founder-voice newsletters.
- **The Main + Roundup:** One substantial piece (300-800 words) followed by 3-5 shorter items (links, observations, recommendations). Best for curated newsletters.
- **The Insight Stack:** 3-5 related insights on one theme, each developed in a short paragraph. Best for analytical newsletters.

**The Close.** Emails benefit from a clear, personal-feeling sign-off. Not "Best regards" — something that matches the newsletter's voice. One CTA maximum. The CTA should be the natural next step from the content (reply, read more, try something). Not a generic "Visit our website."

### Pacing logic
Email pacing is more relaxed than social but not infinite. Front-load the most compelling content. The reader's attention decays across the email, so the best insight should be in the first third. Use formatting (bold key phrases, short paragraphs, occasional one-line paragraphs) to create scan-ability for readers who skim before committing to full reading.

### Closing mechanics
Newsletters build relationships through consistency over time. The close should create a sense of regularity — the reader should know when the next issue arrives and anticipate it. A personal sign-off, a recurring closing element, or a forward-looking tease can create this continuity. Reply prompts ("Hit reply and tell me...") build two-way relationship when used sparingly and genuinely.

### Common structural mistakes
- The Newsletter Template: predictable structure every issue (same sections, same headers, same energy). Consistency of quality, not consistency of format.
- The Link Dump: a newsletter that's just a list of links with one-line descriptions. No voice, no curation perspective, no reason this newsletter exists versus an RSS feed.
- The Sales Email Disguised as Newsletter: promotional content dressed up as editorial. Subscribers signed up for value, not advertising.
- The Throat-Clear Opening: "Welcome to Issue #47 of our monthly newsletter!" — nobody cares about the issue number. Start with the content.

### Benchmarks
- Subject line: 4-9 words. Under 50 characters.
- Body length: 500-1,500 words for primary content. Up to 2,500 for deep-dive issues.
- Paragraphs: 1-3 sentences. Email formatting makes short paragraphs even more important than social.
- Links: 1-3 for single-story emails. 3-7 for roundup formats. Each link should have a genuine reason to click.
- Frequency: weekly or bi-weekly. Monthly is too infrequent to build habit. Daily requires exceptional content quality.

## How to Use This Library

**Format selection is upstream of generation.** Before the Worker generates, the system should select the optimal format for the idea — not default to a standard format. The format should serve the idea, not the other way. A strong insight that needs sequential build-up → carousel. A quick provocation → static + caption. A detailed argument → LinkedIn text post. A visual demonstration → reel. The Format Structure Patterns help the system make this choice and then execute within the chosen format's architecture.

**Structure patterns are constraints, not recipes.** The anatomy sections describe what each section of a format needs to accomplish, not what it should say. "Slide 1 must create a gap" is a constraint. "Slide 1 should say 'Did you know...'" is a recipe. The Worker uses constraints. It never uses recipes.

**Cross-reference with Anti-Pattern Library.** Each format pattern names the anti-patterns most likely to appear in that format. The Boss should audit format-specific structural integrity: is this carousel actually built like a strong carousel (Format #01), or does it match the Everything Carousel anti-pattern (#08)? The structural patterns and anti-patterns are designed to work as complementary audit layers.

**Platform adaptation.** These patterns are format-specific, not platform-specific. A carousel is a carousel whether it's on Instagram or LinkedIn. But platform context changes execution details: LinkedIn carousels tolerate more text per slide, Instagram carousels need stronger visual hooks, and so on. Platform-specific adjustments come from the Platform Playbooks already in the Brand OS. Format Structure Patterns handle the architecture. Platform Playbooks handle the local adaptation.

**The library is V1.** Add format patterns as new formats emerge or existing formats evolve. Threads on Bluesky, carousels on TikTok, Notes on Instagram — each will need its own structural pattern when it becomes a meaningful format for the brands you serve.
