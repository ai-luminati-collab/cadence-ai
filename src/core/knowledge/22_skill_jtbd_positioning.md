# Skill Framework: JTBD-Driven Positioning
> Type: Thinking Methodology (Step-by-step)
> When to use: Strategy generation, brand positioning, competitive analysis
> Confidence: High

---

## Mandatory Thinking Process

When generating strategy or positioning for ANY brand, work through these steps IN ORDER. Do not skip steps.

### Step 1: Identify the Job
Ask: "What progress is the buyer trying to make in their life/work?"
- Functional dimension: What task needs doing?
- Emotional dimension: How do they want to feel?
- Social dimension: How do they want to be perceived?
- Write the job statement: "When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

### Step 2: Map the Forces of Progress
For EVERY target audience segment, map all four forces:
- PUSH: What's making them dissatisfied with their current solution?
- PULL: What's attracting them to new solutions?
- ANXIETY: What fears do they have about switching?
- HABIT: What keeps them stuck with the status quo?

The strongest messaging attacks PUSH + PULL while neutralizing ANXIETY + HABIT.

### Step 3: Find the Competing Alternatives
The real competitor is NOT always the obvious one. Ask:
- "What would they do if our product didn't exist?"
- Non-consumption IS a competitor (doing nothing, manual workaround, spreadsheet)
- The competitive alternative defines the category, not the other way around

### Step 4: Isolate the Unique Value
Based on the job + competing alternatives:
- What can ONLY this brand deliver that the alternatives cannot?
- Strip away everything the competitors also claim
- What remains is the positioning — test it: "Only [brand] does [X] because [proof]"

### Step 5: Validate Against Sharp's Laws
Before finalizing, pressure-test:
- Does this increase mental availability? (Will people think of this brand in buying moments?)
- Does this increase physical availability? (Can they easily find and buy?)
- Is this distinctive, not just differentiated? (Distinctive assets > rational USPs)
- Would a light buyer understand this in 3 seconds?

---

## Anti-Patterns to Block
- "We're the best quality" → Not a position. What specifically?
- "We're for everyone" → No one. Pick a job, not a demographic.
- Positioning that requires explanation → If you need a paragraph, it's wrong.
- Category-first thinking → Start from the job, not the shelf.
