# Skill Framework: Competitive Warfare & Market Intelligence
> Type: Thinking Methodology (Step-by-step)
> When to use: Competitor analysis, strategy generation, positioning decisions
> Confidence: High

---

## Mandatory Competitive Analysis Process

### Step 1: Map the Battlefield
Do NOT start with "who are the competitors." Start with:
- What JOB is the customer hiring a solution for?
- Who else gets hired for that same job? (Direct + indirect + non-consumption)
- Categorize: Direct competitors (same product/market), Indirect (different product, same job), Substitutes (different category entirely)

### Step 2: Analyze Competitor Positioning (Not Just Features)
For each competitor, extract:
- **Positioning statement:** What do they claim to be? For whom?
- **Proof points:** What evidence do they show? (Case studies, numbers, logos)
- **Content strategy:** What do they post? What pillars? What formats? What frequency?
- **Tone/voice:** How do they sound? Professional? Edgy? Educational?
- **Weakness signals:** What do their customers complain about? (Reviews, social comments, Reddit)
- **Pricing position:** Premium, value, freemium? What does price signal about their strategy?

### Step 3: Find the Strategic Gap
Use the Competitive Gap Matrix:
| What competitors ALL do | What competitors ALL ignore |
|---|---|
| This is table stakes — you must match | This is your opportunity space |

| What only 1 competitor does well | What everyone does poorly |
|---|---|
| Differentiation risk — don't copy, find your own | Disruption opportunity — own this |

The most valuable space is: **What the market ignores but the customer needs.**

### Step 4: Choose Your Warfare Strategy
Based on market position, select ONE:

**Flanking (Best for challengers)**
- Attack where competitors are NOT — find an uncontested dimension
- Example: If everyone competes on features, compete on simplicity
- Content angle: "While everyone's doing X, we asked: what if Y?"

**Guerrilla (Best for small/new brands)**
- Don't fight the big players on their terms
- Own a tiny niche so completely that you're the default
- Content angle: Hyper-specific, community-driven, personality-led

**Offensive (Best for #2 player)**
- Find the leader's greatest strength and attack the weakness inherent in it
- Big company = slow. Feature-rich = complex. Established = legacy/boring
- Content angle: Direct comparison, bold claims, "we built what [leader] should have"

**Defensive (Best for market leaders)**
- Block challenger moves before they gain momentum
- Continuously innovate to make your own product obsolete before someone else does
- Content angle: Thought leadership, category definition, platform moves

### Step 5: Competitive Content Playbook
Turn the analysis into content actions:
- **React posts:** When a competitor makes a move, respond within 48 hours
- **Counter-positioning posts:** Directly contrast your approach vs the market default
- **Stolen audience posts:** Target competitors' audience pain points they're ignoring
- **Category-defining posts:** If you're creating a new category, educate the market on why it matters

---

## Rules
- Never mention competitors by name in social content (looks insecure)
- Always position AGAINST a behavior or approach, not against a brand
- Update competitive analysis monthly — markets move fast
- The best competitive strategy is being so different that comparison becomes irrelevant
