## Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning (STP)

Segmentation is the process of dividing a broad market into distinct subgroups based on shared characteristics. The four primary bases are geographic (region, city size, density, climate), demographic (age, income, gender, occupation, education, family size), psychographic (lifestyle, personality, values, attitudes), and behavioral (usage rate, loyalty status, occasions, benefits sought, buyer readiness stage).

Targeting follows segmentation and requires evaluating each segment for size, growth potential, structural attractiveness, and alignment with company objectives and resources. There are five targeting strategies: single-segment concentration, selective specialization, product specialization, market specialization, and full market coverage (differentiated or undifferentiated).

Positioning is the act of designing a company's offering and image to occupy a distinct place in the target market's mind relative to competing products. A positioning statement follows the structure: For [target segment] who [need/opportunity], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]. The strongest positions are built on a single, ownable attribute. Trying to claim too many attributes dilutes the position.

Key frameworks from the canon:
- Kotler's Marketing Management: The STP process is the strategic backbone of modern marketing. All tactical decisions (4Ps, communications, pricing) flow from STP.
- Ries & Trout's Positioning: The battle for the customer's mind. Being first in a category matters more than being better. If you can't be first in an existing category, create a new one you can be first in.
- Byron Sharp's How Brands Grow: Challenges traditional segmentation-heavy approaches. Argues that brand growth comes primarily from increasing mental availability (being thought of in buying situations) and physical availability (being easy to find and buy), not from targeting narrow segments. Most brand buyers are light buyers. Loyalty is a function of market share, not a lever to pull.
- April Dunford's Obviously Awesome: Positioning is not messaging. It is a deliberate choice about competitive alternatives, unique attributes, the value those attributes enable, target customer characteristics, and market category. Mispositioned products fail regardless of quality.

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

People don't buy products; they hire them to accomplish a job. A "job" is the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Jobs have functional, emotional, and social dimensions. The famous example: people don't buy a quarter-inch drill; they buy a quarter-inch hole. And even deeper: they buy the shelf they want to hang.

JTBD interviews focus on the timeline of a purchase: first thought, passive looking, active looking, deciding, consuming, and satisfaction/dissatisfaction. The "forces of progress" model identifies four forces acting on a switch: push of the current situation (dissatisfaction), pull of the new solution (attraction), anxiety of the new solution (fear of change), and habit of the present (comfort with status quo). All four must be accounted for in messaging and product design.

## Pricing Psychology

Key principles: Anchoring (the first price a buyer sees becomes the reference point), charm pricing (prices ending in 9 convert better for emotional purchases; round numbers convert better for luxury/emotional goods), decoy effect (introducing a third inferior option to make the target option look better), price framing (per day vs. per year changes perceived cost), bundling (reduces the pain of paying for individual items), and loss aversion (people feel losses roughly twice as powerfully as equivalent gains).

## Brand Architecture

Brand architecture defines the relationship between a parent brand, sub-brands, and product brands. The three primary models are branded house (single master brand, e.g., Google), house of brands (distinct brands under a corporate umbrella, e.g., P&G), and endorsed brands (sub-brands endorsed by the parent, e.g., Courtyard by Marriott). Hybrid architectures are increasingly common.

## Funnel Fundamentals

The classic funnel stages are Awareness, Interest, Consideration, Intent, Evaluation, and Purchase. Post-purchase stages (Loyalty, Advocacy) are equally critical. Modern funnels are non-linear and often described as loops or flywheels.

Key metrics by stage:
- Top of funnel: Reach, impressions, brand recall, aided/unaided awareness
- Middle of funnel: Engagement rate, click-through rate, content consumption, email open/click rates, lead quality
- Bottom of funnel: Conversion rate, cost per acquisition, ROAS, average order value
- Post-purchase: Repeat purchase rate, customer lifetime value, Net Promoter Score, referral rate, churn rate

## Consumer Behavior Essentials

Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence: Reciprocity (give to receive), Commitment/Consistency (small yeses lead to big yeses), Social Proof (we follow what others do), Authority (we trust experts), Liking (we buy from people we like), Scarcity (we want what is limited).

Seth Godin's Permission Marketing: Interrupt-based marketing is dying. Earn attention by offering anticipated, personal, and relevant messages to people who actually want to receive them. Build a permission asset (your email list, your subscribers) as a core business strategy.

Seth Godin's This Is Marketing: Marketing is not about the stuff you make. It is about the stories you tell, the change you seek to make, and the people you serve. Find the smallest viable audience and serve them so well they tell others.

Jonah Berger's Contagious (STEPPS): Six principles of viral spread: Social Currency (sharing makes people look good), Triggers (environmental cues remind people of the product), Emotion (high-arousal emotions drive sharing), Public (observable products get talked about), Practical Value (useful content gets shared), Stories (information embedded in narratives travels further).

## Lifecycle Marketing

Lifecycle marketing maps communications to where a customer is in their relationship with the brand. Key stages and their primary tactics:

- Acquisition: Paid media, SEO, referrals, partnerships
- Activation: Onboarding sequences, first-purchase experience, welcome series
- Retention: Loyalty programs, personalized recommendations, replenishment reminders
- Revenue: Upsell, cross-sell, bundle offers, subscription upgrades
- Referral: Referral programs, review solicitation, user-generated content campaigns
- Reactivation: Win-back flows, sunset sequences, re-permission campaigns
